A walk through Sinharaja Forest Reserve, Srilanka

Timeline : 2018 January
Sri Lanka Asia Full Day Forest Trekking

I had imagined this drop-shaped island country always to have been green; a green dot floating in the endless blue waters of the mighty Indian Ocean. I would like to believe that it was once covered with dense, impenetrable tropical rainforests, with inneumerable waterfalls, countless streams flowing through. It was as though in an effort to recreate this lost experience, this missed opportunity, that I chose to trek through the Sinharaja Forest Reserve as one of my first experiences in Sri Lanka. It was probably my attempt in holding on to the last remaining patch of pristine primary forests that have fast disappeared everywhere else in the South. This dangerous, dark and mysterious forest has been a legends for centuries, once believed to have been home to the “Sinha”, the lion, although I was not convinced about that completely. I had to be careful while treading this ancient land and witness history, lest I cause any harm unknowingly to the precious vegetation and its delicate ecosystem. It was with this in mind that I had chosen our guide, Pali, who was well-respected as a local guide and naturalist.

When I finally met him, after my long, overnight journey by flight and then car from India, all the way down South, I was reassured by his warm smile and friendly welcome to his homestay. With over 30 years of knowing these forests intimately, his child-like enthusiasm when he spoke of his time in the forests was heartening. I felt that the forest was his home, and he was proud to share it with us for a brief period.

We set off the next morning in a jeep to the entrance of the Sinharaja Forest Reserve, now a UNESCO world heritage site. The mist-covered path was through some settlements and cultivation. As we drove, I could see how the forest has been encroached, inch by inch, each year, with extensive human activity so close by. Located in the South west lowland wet zone of Sri Lanka, these rainforests have been a lifeline to the surrounding villages, in terms of water supply and forest produce. Once spread far and wide, the rainforests are now squeezed into a 7km by 21 km area and is constantly under threat by loggers and poachers, although protected on paper. I could understand the vulnerability of this precious forest as Pali explained about the threats and pressures these trees face each day. We had reached early and the guard post at the entrace was still unoccupied.

Loading salt into our socks, which was Pali’s trusted method of keeping away leeches, which were plenty here, we set off on the trail. The path went through some really large trees right from the entrance. Just a few minutes into our walk, I noticed the darkness. The tall trees with their canopies had almost completely cut off sunlight. And I could hear the unmistakable forest sounds surrounded us as we walked. Known as the most dense rainforests in the whole of Asia, the amount of life found was astounding. Every tree had intertwined vines, creepers, epiphytes, moss growing on them, there was barely any separation, with dense undergrowth, I could feel the presence of frogs, insects and probably snakes at every step. More than 60% of the species of amphibians, reptiles, birds and insects found here are endemic. The darkness and density of the forest makes it very difficult to spot wildlife, although I could hear numerous bird calls. Pali tried to show us some of the endemic species, we came across a tree frog on one of the fallen trees. This massive tree that was lying in our path, was supporting so much life even after its death.

We stopped at a specific point, where he felt something move under the leaves. The forest floor was covered with fallen leaves and he suspected it could be a pit viper, although I could not get a chance to see it clearly. The tall trees supported amazing amount of bird life and it was almost impossible to spot them in the darkness. It had started to rain a little and we continued our walk under the dense canopy. The forests are covered by mist and fog for most part of the year, with a wet climate almost year round. Just outside the forests, it could have been bright and sunny; the microclimate created by these trees was truly remarkable. The path slowly ascends and takes us to even denser parts of the rainforest. It was impossible to tread this path without a guide; the probability of getting lost was so high. Pali was careful to make sure that we walked only on the trail, to ensure that the vegetation is not disturbed. I could see that he cared for every tree, every little creature and treated them with love and respect. I felt he was like this child who was eager to show his priced possessions, yet cautious enough to not give it completely in your hand, in the fear of damaging it.

We heard some loud sound echo across the forest, it were monkeys. I got excited when Pali mentioned that it was the purple faced monkey, which is endemic to this forest. Through my binoculars, with much effort, I could spot this dark coloured monkey and her young one clinging to it. Her face was dark, a little purple probably, giving it the name. She was high in the canopy with very little light around, spoiling my efforts at getting a good picture. I was satisfied watching her through my binoculars. Having walked for almost an hour and a half, it was time for a break.

We climbed a little to reach some rocks and I could hear the sound of flowing water. Just then, Pali asked if we were ready for a swim. Before I could answer, he was walked quickly towards the rocks and in this clearing, there was a beautiful waterfall at some distance, with some smaller water falls along the stream. The water was cool and clear. I sat there on the rocks with my tired feet in the cool waters and enjoying getting nibbled by the numerous fish that were eagerly looking forward for a snack. Pali swam in the waters and came back refreshed. There was noone around except for our small group. We found this sun kissed spot a nice place for our lunch, which was packed for all of us in the morning. Sitting there in this piece of heaven on earth, I felt lucky to have been part of this precious piece of history that is believed to go all the way back to the days of Gondwananland.
