Glacier Tour, Seward, Alaska
Glacier Tour, Seward, Alaska

Timeline : 2018 August
United States of America Alaska North America Half Day Wildlife Nature Boat

A bald eagle swooped in very close to our boat as we set off. The sun was struggling to shine through the dense grey clouds that threatened to pour down anytime. To be sailing off into the icy cold waters of the Pacific, with limited visibility, in the hope of seeing some glaciers, started to seem a bit ambitious. The misty cold morning confined most of us to the cozy interior of the boat, with little to look forward to other than this bald eagle and a friendly otter that did not seem to mind either our boat or the ice cold water. We were in the southern tip of the beautiful piece of land whose geography was aligned with Canada, yet politically, it belonged to the rather far United Stated: Alaska. Seward, a commercial fishing town situated on the Resurrection Bay, is famous for its annual Salmon run. It developed into a tourist hotspot with most of the cruises, short and long ones, originating from this non-descript town owing to its proximity to the glaciers coupled with the convenience of a railroad terminal and good road connectivity to the capital.

We were the lucky few who had managed to get hold of a tour on one of the last days for the season, before the weather would make it non-feasible. So we had already taken our chances by choosing the tail-end of the season, with unpredictable weather threatening to washout our dream of seeing a glacier. I took heart seeing the enthusiastic kayakers who were undeterred by the weather and were ready to jump into the water at Fox Island, which was our first stop. After dropping off three kayakers and wishing them luck, our boat was ready to venture into the open sea. It started to drizzle just as I caught sight of bright orange beaks of puffins that were bobbing their heads in and out of the water near Fox Island. My hopes started to raise as I started seeing more wildlife, a large group of Stellar Sea Lions that found the weather just perfect to sun themselves on the shore of some of the smaller islands we passed by. Just then, a pair of Dall’s porpoises tiny spouts were spotted in the water, soon followed by a view of their fins. These were much smaller than dolphins.

From here riding the rough waters, we looked forward to the most exciting part of this cruise - the vast glaciers of the NW fjords. From a distance I could see the icy blue mountains starting to appear on the side of our boat, soon growing in size so as to engulf us from three sides. It was the first time that I had seen such a huge glacier up close. Nothing that I had imagined ever came close to the experience of actually seeing one - upto 70 feet of snow, compacted, over several thousands of years, giving them the cool blue colour; the entire mountain felt like it was glowing with this faint blue light. It was evident that these glaciers were receeding; many dried up but still a lot of it left. As our boat got closer, I could see huge blocks of ice floating in the water, occupied by seals that were silently drifting on these icy islands.

Once we approached close enough, the engines were cut and there was a deathly silence. All of us, at once, gaping at this magnificent natural wonder. The silence only broken by the sudden cracking sounds that would randomly appear breaking off huge chunks of ice that would then crash into the water. At certain places, a flow of icy water would erupt, like a waterfall, leading sometimes to a crack in the ice. I could watch this phenomenon forever, it was one of the most fascinating action sequences of nature, yet happening continously, slowly and silently. I would look for the thin stream of water flow to catch the breaking of the ice falling into water. The mixed feeling of excitement of seeing such a phenomenon coupled with the sadness of the frequency with the knowledge that it was happening at such a fast rate that would soon cause these mountains to vanish. Sometimes I would be looking in some direction, and hear a crashing sound elsewhere, only catching the falling of the ice into the cold water. The calls of seagulls heightening the effect of this deathly sequence.

The few minutes that we spent, mostly watching this unbelievable sights and sounds, sometimes trying to photograph it, felt like hours, with time stretching to eternity, elongating and etching this in my memory forever. We hadn’t realized the weather had got worse with a storm threatening to break any moment. It was soon time to turn around and leave the company of these towering blocks of ice, with a hope that they would somehow continue to exist forever. On of our way back, our captain announced that she could spot some whale activity, which made all of us excited and run to the deck braving the rain. It could have been a grey whale, I could catch the tail fin as it splashed into the water.

Then our boat meandered through some dreamy landscapes; mounds of green islands floating in water, in the mist. It was also time to see some puffins and other wildlife up close as our boat passed close to these green islands. I could identify some: tufted puffins, common murre and sea lions. The boat slowed down as it navigated the narrow path between two green islands, giving us an excellent photo opportunity with the birds. Despite the gloomy weather and the rough waters, I was satisfied with the sightings and the unparalleled experience of standing in front of a glacier encompassed with the deathly silence, that none of the videos that I took could ever justify.
