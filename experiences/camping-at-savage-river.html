Camping next to Savage river, Denali National Park
Camping next to Savage river, Denali National Park

Experienced in : 2018 August
United States of America Alaska North America Multi Day River Nature Wildlife

The weather was perfect when we entered Denali. The sun was bright and our bus made a special stop on the way to see the majestic mountain because it is not everyday that she presents herself in such glorious view. After completing the mandatory registration and collecting the campsite markers, I got on the park bus that would take me to the campsite, Savage River. It was the farthest point in the park route that allows private vehicles, although we were not driving, so took the bus. The campsite was basic yet organized. We chose the spot that we liked and started to pitch our tent. This would be our home for the next couple of days in Denali.

We could hear the river roar beyond the campground, so as soon as our tent was ready, we walked to the riverbank to get a view of the savage river. The best part about Denali was that it was remote, we were in wilderness, amidst wildlife that roamed freely and fearlessly. To camp here was one of the best decisions. Savage river is a smaller campground compared to Riley Creek and does not have anything much other than dry toilets, a covered dining area, and dumpsters. Everything edible had to be safeguarded in a store room with our names on it to keep away the bears. It was quite cold towards evening and it started to drizzle. Nights were freezing, I could see the frost form on our tent. I woke up in the middle of the night to try my luck with the northern lights. I could barely stand two minutes outside my tent, even with thermals, jackets, gloves, cap and boots. The sky was downcast, starless. To get out in the open, to hear the sounds of wilderness, to feel one with the elements, even the biting cold, the smoke generated from my breath, I would cherish every moment.

Taking a short hike into the wilderness, on one of the several trails near the campground, in just a few minutes I was transported to a different world. Vast green plains, with shadows of clouds on the meadows, huge mountains covered by green, everything in Denali was huge, dwarfing the massive creatures like the black bear and moose. In just about an hour, I had spotted several small birds,a ptarmigan ran into the bushes, a snowshoe hare just crossed our path. Eagles flew high above in the blue sky. I could almost feel that a bear would be standing just around the bend in the trail. There was so much talk about bears and bear spray, how to avoid a bear attack, that everywhere I went in the park, the elusive bear never left the back of my mind!

After a whole day of excellent wildlife viewing from the bus, all I was looking for was to crawl into my sleeping bag and slip into deep sleep. At the campsite, we saw a huge owl perched on the roof of the dining area. It did not seem to be disturbed by our movements, although we tried our best to keep a distance. It was a massive great horned owl. The campground was as much his as it was mine. Still, it felt exciting to share the campground with such a beautiful creature. Leaving it in peace to do its owlish things, we took a short walk down to the river. From our campsite, I could hear the roaring river, wide, with pebble strewn shores and greying glacial water flowing in full force. It was a perfect setting for a campground. I stood there for several minutes mesmerised by the beauty; Denali’s beauty slowly revealed itself in every bush, every cloud, every pebble that I came across.
