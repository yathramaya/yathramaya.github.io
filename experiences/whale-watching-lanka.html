Whale Watching at Mirissa, Sri Lanka
Whale Watching at Mirissa, Sri Lanka

Timeline : 2018 January
Sri Lanka Asia Half Day Wildlife Boat

This was nothing close to what I had ever imagined. The boat was rather small. The waves were menacingly rough, challenging to throw the boat off balance at the slightest opportunity. There were about 20 people on the boat, most of them in various stages of sea sickness. The lucky ones, like myself, who were miraculously unaffected by the rough sea, were on the deck with our eyes glued to the waters. My ears were cautiously listening to our captain for any sightings. He, on the other hand, was enjoying this ride on the bumpy waters. Having grown up on the seas fishing, he was in his elements. I had to hold on firmly to the railings of the boat to avoid getting tossed overboard.

There it was. The unmistakable spout. A sure giveaway to the presence of this beautiful creature. We counted till 10 and then, she came out, I caught a glimpse of her fin and then she was gone. I had travelled to the southern- most edge of SriLanka and ventured into the Indian Ocean to see one of the most marvellous creatures on the planet. An adult blue whale is about 25 metres in length! Longer than our boat. The next few hours were spent hearing “oohs” and “aaahs”, we were lucky to get several sightings. One of them was extremely close to our boat, I could see the entire body of the animal, almost in its full length come out and disappear alongside our boat. Of course, none of this resulted in great photographs as I soon realized that watching a whale and taking a picture, with the right timing is almost impossible. We can never be sure where it would pop out. And unless we are extremely lucky to point our camera in the exact same direction, getting the focus right, it is a difficult task. And the excitement did not allow me to stay calm enough to keep my hands steady.

It was as though I was under a spell. Everything about this trip felt surreal- the boat, the rough sea, the whales swimming around, it was as though I was teleported into some far away land, swept away by the waves, far away from human habitation. Only when our captain called out that it was way past noon and the sea was about to get even rougher that this spell was broken and I woke up to reality. It was already six hours since we started. With all the tossing around, I was not exhausted. I felt it was over too soon. I would have liked to swim along these gentle giants for some more time, drift away from the harsh realities of life, get lost into the deep, unending waters, never to be found again.

Whale watching is not always enjoyable. I had read horror stories of terrible captains and crew members who were abusive, who did not care for the passengers. Moreover, what terrified me was stories about how they would chase the whales into coves, harass them, go extremely close to them, without any care for their feelings. Whales are very shy, gentle creatures and can get extremely stressed with such interference, especially if they have calves swimming along. I had spent most of my research time trying to find a boat that would not do any of this. And, I was lucky to come across this too-good-to-be-true company, run by a spirited bunch of young men, that did not carry any review less than five stars. Generally, something would have gone wrong and there would be bad reviews, especially in cases of sea-sickness. I took a chance and booked it, considering this was the only company to have such an impeccable record. And I could see why. The crew was extremely helpful to all passengers, treated everyone with respect, kept the humor going as it can be very difficult to manage a bunch of sea-sick strangers on unchartered territory. And these young men did everything they could to make us comfortable. The captain, who was also the owner of the boat, was very careful to not disturb the animals. He would drive to places where he felt they would be present, without chasing them, without going too close, allowing them to come nearby and explore us, giving them time to go where they wanted, and graciously following them at a distance. He was one with them. He treated them like his family and not like a commodity to be packaged and sold. And, I could see that he truly enjoyed the sea, the animals. This was the singlemost factor that put me at ease. Such trips can be excellent or terrible, and for me, luckily it was an experience I would cherish forever.
