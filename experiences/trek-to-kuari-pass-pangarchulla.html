A Trek to Kuari Pass and Pangarchulla, Uttarakhand
A Trek to Kuari Pass and Pangarchulla, Uttarakhand

Experienced in : 2019 May
India Asia Multi Day Mountain Trekking

My back was unaccustomed to the heavy load. It was more than a year since I had been on any serious multi-day hike. And, even though we had hired a couple of porters and a guide to carry the camping equipment and the cooking essentials, even the personal items that I had signed up to haul was seeming heavier than I had imagined. The moment I got out of the jeep and started walking through the narrow lanes of the village Dhak, two thoughts were on my mind. One, that for the next seven days, it would just be seven of us in the midst of the mighty Himalayas, far away from civilization and for the first time, living out of a tent. The other, was the immediate nagging thought of the backpack that was dragging me down and slowing my pace. I adjusted the straps of the backpack, tried to take a deep breath and start climbing the steep steps that were carved out of mud and laden with rounded boulders. My heart was pounding and I could hear nothing but my footsteps and my heartbeat for stretches of time. The feeling that I always experience at the beginning of a trek - tired limbs that have been shocked out of their comfort of sitting in a moving vehicle, reluctant, hesitant mind that is anxious about the great distances ahead and, an excitement of being one with nature, the adrenaline rush of being subjected to the ultimate test of one’s endurance.

I could see a small stream flowing through this village, refreshing, with vegetables grown on either side of the stream in small plots of land. The people were probably used to trekkers, so did not pay much attention to our group. Small children playing on the front yard of their homes gave us stealing glances and shy smiles, without directly making eye contact. I generally smiled at them as I walked by. Our guide, Dinesh, was a local, he seemed to know their language and made small conversations as we passed by a few houses. Two others walking with us were also from the same village and they joined our trek directly from the village. I could see that the children were used to some goodies that trekkers had spoilt them with, they were expecting snacks or money from us, which we politely side-stepped with help of Dinesh. Having been on treks and faced with this dilemma, knowing fully the repurcussions of displaying our gear in front of unassuming villagers, I had overcome this awkward situtation and the ensuing guilt by consoling myself that I was doing the right thing under the circumstances, which was to not hand out freebies to children.

Some of these houses looked quite old, with carved wooden pillars, and they all had similar roofing of flat tiles. There were a few new houses getting constructed, with stone and mud walls and sloping roofs. The village itself was relatively clean except for the plastic litter that was strewn around the shops and the water sources, which I am sure was a result of the increasing trekker traffic in this area. We continued our walk, and in about twenty minutes, we were out of the village and into a wooded area. The trail from here was going up a hill, so we climbed, under the tree cover. It was around 10 in the morning and we had set off after a good breakfast. The hike itself had started to become enjoyable and I was getting used to my clothes, my jacket, my shoes and of course, my backpack. These would be my companions for the next few days. I remembered how enjoyable the experience of trekking was; the tiredness, the aching muscles, the occassional cool sip of water, the short breaks to admire an insect or a fallen leaf or butterfly, the keeping one foot in front of another, this rhythm was something I was looking forward to. The climb itself was quite steep, although the path was meandering around the hill, some short-cut sections cut through to gain time was making me more tired. But it was an easy trek, by my standards.

The trees were old, mostly Walnut trees, Akrot in local language, with some of them several feet wide with their roots spread for several meters all around. Some of these roots provided comfortable seating during breaks in the shade of these large trees. It was not the season for the nuts, hence, we just found a few empty walnut shells at the base of the trees, discarded by squirrels, probably. A stream was flowing at a distance below, probably the upper parts of the stream I saw in the village. We were soon approaching the meadows that were beyond this tree cover. The first day of walk was neither strenous, nor long, probably lasted a couple of hours at a comfortable pace, we would soon approach our first campsite at Akrotghetta. Walking through the meadows, the view opened up and for the first time and I realized we were surrounded by snow capped mountains. The imposing presence of Dronagiri to the North would stay with us during the entire duration of the trek.

Our efficient and thoughtful porters and cook had walked a few paces ahead and had already started setting up our tents. We chose a good, flat place for the tent and pitched ours. They took charge of the kitchen and throughout this trek we would enjoy freshly cooked, hearty meals, tea and snacks. Having been on treks that were either in lodges, with fixed meals, or on our own, with mostly dry cold food and basic khichidi and soups, this arrangement of accompanying cooks was sort of a luxury we had not accounted for, but would appreciate and enjoy every bit. Apart from our tent for three, there was a kitchen tent, which also served as sleeping place for two of them, and another dining tent, which were used by two more to sleep at night. The arrangements were simple and served the basic purpose it was intended for.

Since it was still half-day, I was looking forward for some short walks around the campsite and indulge in photography of the snow covered peaks and the beautiful grasslands. The sun was still up, so I did not feel very cold yet. Around 4pm, the winds started blowing, this brought some chills. The sleeping bags provided was the life-saver, it was so warm that once I got into it, it took just ten minutes for me to feel comfortable, and sometimes, warm too. The weather was pleasant and the skies clear. Towards evening, a couple of kids from the village walked up to our tent and peered inside out of curiosity. I just smiled at them as I did not speak their language. They just hung around the kitchen tent for their share of biscuits and then ran downhill. It seemed like they exactly knew the tea-time of trekking groups and arrived just in time for the snacks.

Our trekking guide, Dinesh, was young, friendly and playful, he was not very strict and was eager to ensure that we were satisfied with all the arrangements. However, once we started discussing the next day’s plan and schedule, his face would stiffen, he would put up a stern expression and speak to us very seriously. The playfulness displayed a few seconds ago would completely vanish, replaced by maturity and discipline. This was something that I admired in him, because, I wanted to be sure that we had entrusted our lives with someone who knew well what he was doing. Dinesh’s brother Rakesh, who was one of the porters and had generally accompanied us to learn the drill from his brother, was soft- spoken and smiling, he served us all our meals and helped around in the kitchen. Birender, a shy person, who had not spoken much to us, other than to say his name, would accompany us on our further climbs. Kapil, the cook and Rakesh would stay back and ensure that the campsite was taken care of and the meals were ready before we started the trek and before we arrived at the campsite after the trek each day.

We later would learn that the difficulty and the duration of trekking days were not evenly distributed over all seven days. The first two days would be easy; so were the last two days. All the difficulty was concentrated on the two key days where we would trek till Kuari Pass and Pangarchulla Summit. And it was not progressive either. The key trek days would start from the same point and then go all the way and return to the base camp, which did not change since the second day. So effectively, we would walk the same stretch twice over, since the first few hours of these two days would be common. For now, we were taking it as it comes and not stress on the summit, for every moment in the company of these majestic snow clad mountains was cherishable.

The next day, being another short trek to the second base camp, we were in no hurry to pack up and leave. I however woke up early to see the sun rise and catch the first light on the face of the mountains. We were offered hot tea and biscuits, which was welcomed by everyone. We took time to change into trekking clothes, pack the sleeping bags, pack our backpacks and then proceed for breakfast. I was surprised to be served aloo parantha, something that I had got used to during my stay in Joshimath, however, was not expecting them to arrange it during a trek! Kapil was an excellent cook, who ensured that we had variety on each day of the trek and everything tasted fresh and delicious. I was wondering how they were managing to procure fresh vegetables, provisions for all our elaborate meals. I later realized that we were hardly three to four hours from the trail-head and they considered it a stroll to run down and fetch anything that was missed. I was not completely happy with the indulgence, but I was only thankful for their hospitality, as the food kept us in good spirits throughout the trek.

We set off further up the meadows towards a larger spread of grasslands called Bugyal. This was probably the most beautiful, picture perfect campsite I have camped on. It would become our home for the next 4 days, till we complete both the treks. Having got used to the walking and sufficiently warmed up the previous day, I found the second day of trek to be easier. We had the beautiful scenery to distract us as a bonus. The path we were taking was a common route for herders who would take their cattle and sheep up and down everyday from the village below. Closer to our campsite, we saw a large herd of sheep, probably about 400 to 500, accompanied by shepherds and sheep dogs. I saw a sheep giving birth to a young one right in front of us. Some of them were shy and moved away when we were photographing them, while some large ones came closer for inspection. This herd would stay right next to our campsite for the next couple of days. We would hear sheeps bleat all night. The sheep dog would adopt us during our entire stay and sleep just outside our tent, ignoring the sheep.

We later learnt from Dinesh that the actual site, Khullara, was now off-limits for trekkers and the government was not giving permits to camp there. That evening when we took a walk there to see the beautiful grasslands, we would understand why. The grassland was vast, spanning about half a kilometre, a flat land, with slight undulations, but overall even, covered with grass. The landscape was dotted with trees bearing colourful flowers and leaves. A stream with pristine, sparkling water was flowing right in the middle of the meadow, separating it into two. The path passed further right through this beautiful landscape. There was a small stone bridge over the stream at one place. The grassland itself was overused, with patches of brown mud showing where the grass was not growing. I could imagine how this unique ecosystem was disturbed with several tents and hundreds of people walking all over the grass every season. The large trekking groups consist of several tents and accompanying paraphernilia to make trekkers comfortable. All this would put undue load on the grassland ecosystem and slowly, they were dying. The goverment, in a desperate effort to save this unique landscape and maintain it in pristine condition had brought about a ban on camping here. We were happy that these meadows would be free from human footsteps and would continue to look beautiful for years.

This place was cooler than the previous campsite. And we were rewarded with breathtaking views of Neelkanth, Hathi Ghoda and other peaks right outside our tent door. It was a priceless feeling to wake up in the morning, crawl out of the tent and be stunned with snow clad mountains right in front. We encountered another day of clear weather. That night, over a sumptuous dinner of dhal, roti, rice and brinjal curry, we discussed our next day, which would be the Kuari Pass trek. We would wake up early and start off in the dark with packed breakfast and lunch. The winds were strong that night, we hoped for a clear day. The moon had come out when we woke up early the next morning. The snow covered mountains were faintly visible in the moonlight, casting shadows, and everything was still except for some activity in the kitchen tent. We quickly got ready, packed out day packs and got ready for our trek to Kuari Pass.

The initial part of the walk was difficult. Once we went past Khullara, a steep climb over boulders of various sizes made our walk slower and unsteady. I carefully stepped on each stone to assure a firm footing before moving my other foot, and all this maneuver in darkness with headlamps. It was a blessing that we had explored some of this terrain the previous evening during our walk, so we could remember some parts of the trail that were especially tricky. Dinesh and Birender accompanied us, each at either end of the group, making sure that we were catching up. All of us carried light day packs, consisting of mostly gaiters, crampons and our lunch packets. We carried two litres of water each, which we found to be necessary. It was strenous compared to the last couple of days of easy walking through well marked trails. I named this area with the boulders and uneven terrain, with hardly any scenery to enjoy in the darkness, as ‘drudgery area’. In most treks, we tend to encounter atleast one such stretch that is hardly pleasant, extremely difficult and does not reward us with views.

What I was looking foward eagerly was for the sun to rise. We took a short break at an opening to see the sun rise over the magnificent snow covered mountains. It gave us fresh energy to continue the trek. Also now that I could see better, it greatly helped keep the pace. The next section, after the drudgery stretch, was a valley with a stream running through, cutting it into two halves. We crossed this stream over boulders to the right side and started walking in this vast plain. The terrain was easier than the section we had just completed. There were some huge mountains beyond the immediate smaller ones that we were about to climb. The trail crossed the valley and we started climbing steeply along the slopes of a hill, that was covered with boulders, but the snow had mostly melted, leaving behind patches of slush covered with grass. Climbing this led us to another similar mountain that we started to cross. Some of us had got hungry and had munched on the packed food along the way. Then, we made an official stop to eat. The aloo paratha tasted heavenly. By this time we had already ascended more than 200 meters. We would now move progressively to higher altitudes with each crossing of the mountain. We walked along the top ridge of a mountain with brilliant views on either side, with the valley far below us now.

The morning sun had now warmed me sufficiently and I peeled off the warm jacket. The air was thinner and I could breathe more easily. We were promised a clear day and I was thankful for that. Kuari Pass is like a panorama, where you can stand at one point and witness huge peaks all around, like an amphitheatre. Further, we walked on to climb another steep mountain, after which we could see the trail go through snow, we sat at the edge of this mountain, got our gaiters and crampons ready. The next part of the trail would be through snow, this was an experience that I had never had before, and something that I was looking forward to with eagerness and apprehension. The scenery was so beautiful, vast stretches of snow all around, with no trail defined. We could have got easily lost in ten minutes had it not been for our guide. Dinesh recounted stories of trekkers and porters who had got lost in a storm here and it had become dark. They sent one person to descend to the campsite, and to their horror, after two hours, he wandered and reached them, without a clue about where he was. He was just going around in circles, and had believed to be in the direction of the campsite, but in darkness, he lost his way. With such dreadful stories, we continued our hike thanking the weather we had so far.

Walking on snow is most tedious. Firstly, we do not have any idea where we are stepping, it is hard to guage. It can only be through trial and error and experience of several expeditions that we can start to recognize the snow. We were severely dependent on our guides during this exercise. When the snow is soft, we are unsure of how deep our leg will sink, so it is always prudent to secure one foot before engaging the next. If the snow is hardened, then it will slip and on narrow trails over ridges this can be dangerous. All the enjoyment of seeing the snow and the anticipation of walking on it, wearing crampons, disappeared after walking for ten steps. The crampons were invaluable in this whole exercise, I realized. Although it was difficult to walk on surfaces where the snow had melted, like some rocks that we had to navigate in our path in between two stretches of snow, where we still had the crampons on, there was no replacement for a good pair of crampons on snow. With some practice and newfound confidence, I started moving without anxiety, with a surer foot. Although, in tricky situations, Birender was always behind to lend a quick, strong arm to hold on to. It was here that I realized how well equipped he was in dealing with difficult situations and focusing on our safety at all times. Dinesh had to lead the way and find the right path in the snow, because there was no visible trail, he also had to test the surface to see if it slips or whether it will sink into a pit. While he was focused on the fore, Birender ensured all three of us were safe and watching over us if one of us needed any help in navigating some difficult patches. He also opened up and shared some of his stories and trekking experiences.

Most of this part of the trek was not steep, so it was easy on the lungs, so once we got used to the terrain, we could move a little faster. We reached a valley, where the trail would split into two, with the left arm leading towards the Pangarchulla and the right one towards Kuari Pass. Today, we had to take the right arm. There was a small shrine on our path, this was a temple two feet tall built with stones piled up. This was also the earlier advanced base camp for Pangarchulla summit, which we could not take benefit of. We had to trek all the way beyond Khullara where we had camped once again the next day to attempt Pangarchulla, which we felt was unfair, and greatly reduced chances of a successful summit. This campsite was also banned by the Goverment recently.

Beyond this valley of snow, we had one more climb on a snow clad mountain slope to reach probably the highest point in the trek. This place was not Kuari Pass, but it was the high point and the last point on this trail with snow. We took a break here and also found it a nice spot to eat our lunch. However, it was very windy here and some of us had mild symptoms of altitude sickness, like loss of appetite. I was very hungry and was waiting to eat the roti and aloo gobi curry that Kapil had packed for us in an aluminium foil. We also met a local here, who was busy looking for a specific fungi that would fetch a good price in China in the illegal trade. Dinesh told us that in the cold season there would be hundreds of villagers searching for this elusive fungi and they would not reveal whether they found any or not, for the fear of getting robbed. It seems even a gram of this would fetch them thousand of rupees. So they would brave the winter cold and come look for this.

From this point, it was a descent to Kuari Pass, which we reached pretty easily, considering all the hardwork we had put forth till now. We were relaxed and spent a few minutes soaking in the views at this point. The sun was still bright and we were lucky to have a clear view. We could see the valley further down, where the trail would continue. For us, this was the point we would turn back and retrace all the way to the campsite. Although the return would be along the same path, Dinesh wanted to introduce some excitement along the way. We walked till the point where we reached the valley of snow and instead of taking the route we used to climb here, he took us to another point, a high ridge from where he said we could just slide down. I initially thought he was just joking, like he always did. But before we could say anything, he was sprinting ahead of us, with the excitement of a small child. This short-cut, although sounding risky at first, enticed me. My spontaneous self threw all the caution into the wind and signed up to free-board down a steep slope of snow, something that I have never tried before in my life.

The first to go was Dinesh himself, creating a rough path. He landed perfectly, using his legs to slow down towards the end before stopping. I watched carefully and tried to make mental notes; Birender was behind me, giving instructions and last minute tips. He did not sound very pleased at this sudden change of plans. Ratheesh was the next to go and he too did not seem to face any trouble. This gave me a lot of confidence, and I quickly followed the same trajectory and luckily landed safely without any problems. The whole experience lasted a few seconds, but it was enjoyable, like a roller coaster ride. I had not given much thought to what would happen if for some reason I could not break the flow and would just go straight into the valley below. After me, it was Giri’s turn. He started off rather clumsily and by now the ice had hardened a little and slid faster. His weight coupled with this hardening accelerated his slide and he abruptly spun midway, now coming down, to our utter shock, in full speed with his back facing down, with no way to use his legs to slow his fall. Luckily, we managed to stop his fall and rescued him from his accidental fall deeper into the valley. He was visibly shaken by this experience. Birender, after seeing all this, decided not to slide and walked down through an alternative route.

The whole valley to ourselves, without any other trekker in sight, we took our time to enjoy the sunny day, the glistening snow under our boots, the small violet flowers that grew among the green patches. The descent was easier on my lungs, so I was walking faster, however, my knees took most of the impact. It was slippery in certain places, but overall, due to the exhiliration of a completed trek and the general good weather we were blessed with, the walk became enjoyable and relaxed. After a point, when Dinesh was certain that we would find our way back, he asked us to walk at our own pace and reach the campsite at leisure, which I felt was a great offer, since we were in no specific hurry to return. And we didn’t want to slow them down. Within the next five minutes, I saw two tiny heads disappear beyond the next meadow, merge with the landscape against the gigantic mountains and finally become indistinguishable to my eyes. It was getting closer to lunchtime and I could already hear my stomach rumble thinking about the tasty meal that awaited us at the camp.

The beautiful weather that we had during the day was replaced abruptly with overcast skies and terrible cold winds accompanied by rain. During our dinner, we had to take a call whether to summit Pangarchulla the next morning or not. We decided to give it a day’s break and attempt it after a day. Apart from the fact that we were thoroughly tired from the long walk, we wanted to guage the weather that seemed to alternate between sunny and overcast, giving us the hope that the next day would be cloudy, followed by a clear summit day. Atleast, so far, as I had observed, this was the pattern. But nothing in the mountains are predictable. The next morning turned out to be one of the clearest days in the entire trek. We spent the day with photography and lazing around on the heated rocks. We were all visibly tired and could not have attempted Pangarchulla that day. Now, looking back, in the cozy confines of my bed, as I type this, in tropical climate, I would argue that had we pushed ourselves a little, we might have had a comfortable, clear ascent to the summit. However, that was not what my instincts told me at the camp.

Having successfully completed Kuari Pass, and thus 50% of our trek, we felt confident of the next day’s plans, which was to take a diversion to the Pangarchulla advanced base camp and subsequently attempt the summit, hopefully before 12pm. We had given ourselves this deadline based on Dinesh’s past experiences of successful summits. The next morning, having got a good practice of the drill, we promptly got ready and started walking with our headlamps at 3:30am. The weather having been overall chilly the previous night and not having rained much. But I had my own logic (that I kept to myself) that good rains the previous nights always means clearer days subsequently; so I felt that since it did not rain much at night, the day would not be as clear as we would have liked it to be.

As we went through, this time more easily over the drudgery section, over large boulders and slippery rocks, we reached the sunrise point in good time, beating our own previous timing by a good 45 minutes. But what we saw was not promising; the Sun, although risen, was barely visible, concealed by dense clouds. But hoping that the weather might improve as the day progresses, we trekked further, over the huge grasslands, the river basin and began climbing the mountain slopes to the right. A light drizzle that progressed into a light snow fall, by the time we reached the steep ridge. I walked on the ridge with very low visibility, completely losing the person walking right ahead of me into the mist. This not just slowed me down, but sank my hopes of a clear day. By then, I had almost known that we might not be able to get the view that we had dreamed of on the Summit. Yet, keeping my optimism going, led by Dinesh’s encouraging words, I kept the pace.

By the time we climbed the steep mountain that was the crossover to the snow plains, I realized that this day would be very different from our Kuari Pass trek, just two days ago. What might have been an easy-moderate trek started to look difficult now. And challenging. Walking on the snow with almost zero visibility, with no breathtaking views for encouragement, we tried hard to keep up our spirits and not get demotivated. At a distance, when for sometime the mist cleared, I saw another group that was atleast half an hour ahead of us. It looked like they would be attempting the summit, despite the rough weather. Our guide was apprehensive of their decision and felt it was overall a risky idea. I couldn’t agree more. I was thankful to be part of a group that put safety first over success of summitting. Also, we had an unsaid agreement of ‘all or none’, which meant we would never split the group and would attempt only if everyone would be comfortable. These are times when trekking with people we know and trust really made the difference.

By the time we had walked over the snow covered valley and traversed the path where we had skid the previous time, we reached a point from where we could faintly see the ridge of Pangarchulla peak. By this time, a black dog had appeared out of nowhere and had inducted itself part of our trekking group. Standing there, at around 11:30am, we had to take a call whether to push further or turn back. We completely entrusted this decision to our guide. His face assumed a grave expression; he went silent, lost in deep thought. His eyes were following the tiny black dots on the ridge; the advance party that had preceeded us to the summit. From a distance, it looked like a couple of people were stranded at the bottom of the steep ridge, while some more were inching ahead, moving very slowly, almost getting lost into the mist. Dinesh explained to us that it was a very risky walk and the ridge was quite narrow. A small mistake would take them crashing down the steep slope. It did look quite scary. So we took the only logical decision that was possible at that time; to turn back. However, Dinesh and Birender guessed that the other group might need some help, as he guaged that the two people left stranded at the bottom of the ridge might be sick. From this distance, they seemed disoriented and suffering from AMS, which was probably the reason why they were left there. But he was furious that their guide prioritized the rest of the group and chose to lead them to the summit rather than stay with the sick and take them back to safety. So he wanted us to wait while he and Birender would go speak to them.

While we waited, we took pictures of ourselves, with almost nothing in the background except mist. What lightened our moods was a second dog that had joined our group, probably returning from the summit. I was amazed at its agility and how it easily stepped on the snow without worrying. It might have summitted Pangarchulla several times during this season already, almost treating it like a regular morning walk. The two dogs started playing around, chasing each other in the snow. Dinesh returned and was visibly furious, having confirmed his theory on the trekking group. He told us that the guide had deserted them and gone ahead. However, we could see that the group was not successful in its attempt to summit and had returned after waiting on the ridge for a few minutes and giving up.

We began our return journey, with a heavy heart. It felt like it was so near yet so far. That feeling of having almost reached there, with the destination visible and clearly within reach, having cheated by the weather that did not co-operate. This is why I love the mountains. They ultimately decide who should summit and who shouldn’t. AMS, weather, all these might be excuses but the best of the best cannot proceed if the mountain doesn’t wish to receive them. Whether we would have summitted or not, had we pushed on and taken the risk, we do not know. However, what we know for sure is that, there was zero visibility at the top. We would have never got the magnificent view that we had come for. So a mere successful ascent to the top would not have been satisfying to us without the views. The views are what we came for. And that, on this trek, was not revealed to us. We had to respect the mountain for its decision and return. Probably, some other day, we might come back when Pangarchulla calls us again.
